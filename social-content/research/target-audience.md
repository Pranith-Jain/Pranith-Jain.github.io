# Target Audience — Personas

## Persona 1: The Junior SOC Analyst

**Name**: Alex | **Age**: 24 | **Experience**: 0–2 years

**Pain points**:

- Overwhelmed by alert volume, can't triage effectively
- Imposter syndrome — feels unqualified
- Doesn't know which skills to learn next
- Career path unclear — SOC → what?

**What they want**:

- Clear, actionable skill roadmaps
- Tool tutorials that don't assume prior knowledge
- Career advice from someone who's been there
- Validation that their struggles are normal

**Content they engage with**:

- "5 skills every SOC analyst needs" (list)
- "How I went from L1 to DFIR" (story)
- "The one thing I wish I knew as a junior" (curiosity gap)

**Where they hang out**: LinkedIn, Reddit r/cybersecurity, Discord servers

---

## Persona 2: The Mid-Level Detection Engineer

**Name**: Jordan | **Age**: 30 | **Experience**: 3–7 years

**Pain points**:

- Stuck doing reactive alert triage, wants proactive detection
- Hard to justify detection engineering to management
- Open-source tools vs commercial — decision fatigue
- Wants to build a personal brand but doesn't know how

**What they want**:

- Deep technical content (not surface-level)
- Framework guidance (MITRE ATT&CK, Sigma, YARA)
- Real-world examples they can adapt
- Thought leadership positioning

**Content they engage with**:

- "How to write detection rules that actually catch attacks" (tutorial)
- "SIEM vs EDR vs XDR — when to use each" (comparison)
- "The detection engineering maturity model" (framework)

**Where they hang out**: LinkedIn, Twitter/X InfoSec, GitHub

---

## Persona 3: The CISO / Security Director

**Name**: Morgan | **Age**: 42 | **Experience**: 15+ years

**Pain points**:

- Board doesn't understand cybersecurity ROI
- Budget pressure — need to justify every tool
- Talent shortage — can't hire enough people
- Vendor fatigue — everyone's selling "AI-powered" solutions

**What they want**:

- Business-case language, not technical jargon
- Risk quantification frameworks
- Industry benchmarks and peer comparisons
- Strategic trends, not tactical noise

**Content they engage with**:

- "How to present cybersecurity ROI to your board" (how-to)
- "The real cost of a breach in 2026" (data shock)
- "Why your security budget is backwards" (hot take)

**Where they hang out**: LinkedIn (primarily), executive roundtables

---

## Persona 4: The Career Changer

**Name**: Sam | **Age**: 28 | **Experience**: 0 years (transitioning)

**Pain points**:

- No technical background, intimidated by jargon
- Overwhelmed by certification options (CompTIA, GIAC, etc.)
- Imposter syndrome even before starting
- Doesn't know if cybersecurity is right for them

**What they want**:

- Honest, no-BS career guidance
- Clear entry points (SOC, GRC, pentesting, etc.)
- Realistic timelines and expectations
- Encouragement without toxic positivity

**Content they engage with**:

- "Is cybersecurity right for you? (Honest assessment)" (question)
- "From [previous career] to DFIR in 12 months" (story)
- "Certifications that actually matter in 2026" (list)

**Where they hang out**: LinkedIn, Reddit, YouTube, bootcamp communities

---

## Primary Audience (for content strategy)

**80% of content targets**: Junior-to-mid security practitioners (Personas 1 & 2)
**15% of content targets**: Career changers and aspirants (Persona 4)
**5% of content targets**: CISOs and leaders (Persona 3)

**Rationale**: Junior/mid practitioners are the largest audience, most active on social media, and most likely to engage. They also become senior leaders who remember your content.

---

## Positioning Statement

> "I help security practitioners cut through vendor noise and build real-world detection skills — through frameworks, tools, and honest career advice."

This positions the content as:

- **Practitioner-focused** (not vendor-focused)
- **Action-oriented** (not theoretical)
- **Honest** (not hype-driven)
- **Framework-driven** (not random tips)
